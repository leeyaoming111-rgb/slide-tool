# Copy rules — how a finance slide is worded (the Auckland voice)

Distilled from the UoA Case Programme's own material: "The New Auckland Case Model" workbook
(`references/model-reference.xlsx`, the canonical section-by-section script), the Barbie/Mattel
exemplar deck, the Case Finance 101 graphs, and the 2026 competition decks (HKU, Sydney A).
Scope: finance slides. Every rule here is a hard default, like `CHART_RULES.md`.

Goal: the identity of the Auckland model, elevated. Where the source decks are sloppy
(hand-drawn sketches), the *pattern* is kept and the *execution* is standardised.

## 1. Voice

1. **Second person, always.** The deck talks *to* the client: "You will 2.5x your client
   base", "your customers", "your shareholders". Never "the company" or "X should".
2. **Every claim is defensible.** Judges punish numbers that can't be reconstructed (this
   cost a UoA team a trans-Tasman final). Mark estimates explicitly: "est 80%",
   "~50,000 units/yr". If a number can't be defended in Q&A, soften it or cut it.
3. **No empty scene-setting.** Banned: "X is a thriving market leader" and its relatives
   (the model's own changelog: "I will cry if I hear about X being a thriving market
   leader again"). Every sentence either carries a number or sets one up.
4. **No em dashes** in slide copy (house rule, reaffirmed). Colons, commas, semicolons,
   or a new sentence.

## 2. Action titles on finance exhibits

Every title is a full sentence with a verb and **carries the headline number: one metric +
one timeframe**. Three sanctioned grammars:

- **Outcome-from-driver:** "$300m profit in 5 years from home brand expansion"
  = `[metric outcome] in/by [timeframe] from/by/through [driver]`
- **Imperative-with-target:** "Reach over 1,000 stores by 2000",
  "Maintain margins by home-brand value competition"
- **You-will:** "You will grow supply to 525K units by year 5",
  "After Y5 you will 2.5x your client base"

Impact lines prefer **multiples and percentages** ("2.5x", "75% by 2030", "5.4x those
returns"); builds and bridges use absolutes.

## 3. The case metric (what the whole deck is priced in)

5. The bar to clear is always **status quo**: "achieve beyond a status-quo profit of $X
   by 20XX". A strategy is only successful relative to that bar.
6. Scenario endpoints are labelled **"Status Quo 20XX" -> levers -> "Proposed 20XX"**.
   Abbreviate to "SQ" only inside tight chart labels.
7. Metric hierarchy: **profit** (normal company) > **lives changed / people reached**
   (non-profit) > **revenue** (grow-at-all-costs businesses). **Market share is never the
   headline metric.**
8. Waterfall/bridge levers are named with the driver noun, one or two words ("Stores",
   "Margins") or the strategy's name.

## 4. The lever sentence (the strat-to-finance handoff)

9. Every strategy earns its finance slide with one canonical sentence:
   `[Strategy] addresses [insight] by [implementation tease], growing [metric] by
   increasing [lever]`.
   The finance exhibit then shows exactly that lever moving. Model example: "This will
   increase loyalty and thus lifetime spend per customer, driving profit growth through
   higher revenue."
10. Strategies carry **memorable names** (two to four words, often alliterative or a
    portmanteau: "Serve the Curve", "Reach & Teach", "Don't Buy Barbie") and finance
    slides refer to them **by name** in lever labels and legends.

## 5. Show the arithmetic in words

11. Bottom-up builds state their formula in labels: "avg rev/user x # of users = revenue".
    The reader must be able to reconstruct the headline number from the slide alone.
12. Every finance exhibit carries an **Assumptions panel**: short quantified noun phrases
    ("Pre-tax margin of 20%", "300 workshops per centre p.a.", "8.38% WACC", "Terminal
    growth of 1.5%"). Assumptions sit adjacent to the number they support, never on a
    separate slide.
13. Impact totals are phrased as **builds**: "current + directly reached + indirectly
    reached = total".

## 6. Costs and funding get one breath

14. Benefits get the build; **each cost line gets one phrase, funding gets one sentence**
    ("Payback in 20 months", "$115m total, funded from operating cash flow"). Costs are
    on-slide but brief; depth lives in the appendix and Q&A.

## 7. Number style (the elevation layer)

15. Currency: `$26m`, `$1.5b`, `$450k`. Lowercase unit letter, no space, never
    "mill"/"bn"/"K". State the currency once per slide (a header note like "USD"), not on
    every number.
16. Multiples: `2.5x` (lowercase x, one decimal max). Returns: `13.0x ROI`, `26% IRR`.
    Growth: `+12-15% p.a.`
17. Consistent precision within an exhibit (one decimal or whole numbers, not both).
    "est" or "~" marks estimates. Ranges read "X to Y" in prose, `X-Y` in labels.

## 8. Risk and scenario wording

18. Risk slide script: "We have identified X risks and proposed mitigations. The biggest
    is Y; we suggest Z." Present only the biggest risk; the rest are bait for Q&A.
19. Scenarios are labelled S1/S2/S3 or Worst/Base/Best, always anchored against Status Quo.

## 9. Takeaway strips

20. Every analysis exhibit closes with a **so-what phrased as a benefit to "you"**:
    "Serve the Curve reaches 5.5k missing-middle clients by year 5". A takeaway restates
    the implication, never the chart.
